Problem Thesis — Gate Review

The Problem

Due diligence fails because cross-document synthesis of thousands of unstructured pages within deal-driven timelines exceeds the biological limits of human cognition — and every layer of the current system, from VDR storage to organizational structure to deal incentives, is architecturally incapable of closing the gap.

Who Suffers

Deal Teams

PE associates, IB analysts, Big Law attorneys, Big 4 consultants

8–10+ parallel workstreams. 5,000–50,000+ pages per data room. 500+ contracts requiring 3,750 attorney hours for complete review. Traditional DD examines only 5–10% of contracts.

(InvestorDataRooms.com; Spellbook/V7 Labs; Sirion.ai)

Investment Committees

Principals, partners, IC members

The IC memo is the sole forced synthesis point — written by a junior deal team member under time pressure. No platform provides cross-workstream analytical synthesis.

(Stanford/Addepar; Cross-Workstream Synthesis research, 2025)

Portfolio Managers & LPs

Operating partners, fund managers, limited partners

Decisions made on data 3–6 months old. 46% rely exclusively on Excel. 18,000+ companies in exit backlog. Record 110 PE-backed bankruptcies in 2024.

(Preqin; McKinsey; S&P Global)

Target Company Management

CEOs, CFOs, founders preparing for sale or fundraise

CEO/CFO at 30 hours/week during DD. 7–12 parallel buyer workstreams. DD past 90 days drops closure probability below 50%. Business performance visibly suffers.

(SilMinds; Business Sale Report)

Case Studies: When Cross-Reference Fails

Valeant / Salix — $14.5B (2015)

Financial statements showed strong revenue. Wholesaler inventory showed 5–9 months excess. Prescription data showed flat demand. Cross-referencing all three reveals channel stuffing. Allergan’s team caught it and walked away. Valeant’s did not.

GE / Alstom — $10.6B (2015)

Alstom’s own annual report warned of “excess capacity in developed markets.” GE’s models projected robust demand. Result: $23B writedown — more than double the purchase price.

Marriott / Starwood — $13.6B (2016)

Prior breach disclosure, legacy IT documentation, and 383M guest records existed in separate workstreams reviewed by different teams. Result: £18.4M in fines, $1B+ revenue losses.

Bayer / Monsanto — $63B (2018)

Public IARC carcinogen classification + internal MDL discovery documents + DD “limited exclusively to memoranda by a US law firm.” Result: $10B+ settlements, 67,000+ lawsuits.

BofA / Countrywide — $2.5B (2008)

Formal Loan Program Guides vs. internal “shadow guidelines” showing 15% underwriting exceptions. 60-person DD team, 90 days. Result: $60B+ in post-close losses.

Kraft Heinz — 2015 merger

Goodwill allocation increased 4.9% despite declining pro forma sales. SEC revealed $208M in fictitious cost savings. Result: $15.4B impairment.

The EBITDA-multiplier effect: Claims seeking multiple-based damages rose from 5% in 2020 to 32% in 2024 (Fasken/Aon). A $2M error at 10x EBITDA becomes a $20M claim. Every missed cross-reference is exponentially more costly than five years ago.

Why It Persists

VDRs Solved Security, Not Intelligence

Virtual data rooms remain “passive digital filing cabinets” (V7 Labs, 2025). No major VDR offers native cross-document analysis. All four major platforms feature Excel export as a core Q&A capability — an implicit admission their native interfaces are insufficient.

AI Tools Face a “Trust Stack” of Barriers

Stanford RegLab found legal AI tools hallucinate 17–34%. 41% of lawyers require 100% accuracy (Thomson Reuters). Deal data is MNPI. SOC 2 takes 6–12 months; deals close in 4–6 weeks. ABA Opinion 512: uncritical AI reliance is “almost certainly malpractice.” Virtually every first-wave legal AI company has been acquired or shut down: RAVN, eBrevia, Kira, Seal, ROSS, Evisort, Eigen.

Deal Incentives Suppress Disconfirming Evidence

DD “seldom leads managers to kill potential acquisitions, even when the deals are deeply flawed” (Harvard Business Review). No-undisclosed-liabilities claims more than doubled between 2022 and 2024, linked to “decreased buyer diligence in the competitive 2021 M&A market” (SRS Acquiom).

Organizational Design Guarantees Silos

8–10+ parallel workstreams by necessity. No lateral relations exist across them (Galbraith, 1974). The deal coordinator lacks authority to compel cross-workstream sharing. Full-team meetings convene only 2–4 times during the process.

First Principles

P1 — Cognitive Degradation
Human cognitive capacity for cross-referencing information degrades predictably with document volume and time pressure — and expertise does not protect.
83% radiologist miss rate (Drew et al., 2013). 28% reviewer agreement (Roitblat et al., 2010). System 2 preferentially impaired under load (Croskerry). No experience-level protection (Krupat et al., 2017). Working memory ~4±1 items (Cowan, 2001).
→ Solution must augment human processing capacity. The human role must shift from reading/finding to judging/deciding.
P2 — Structurally Unstructured
DD information is structurally unstructured — documents lack machine-readable organization by design, and no team can impose structure fast enough within deal timelines.
500+ contracts at 5–10 hours each (Spellbook/V7 Labs). 174 document types (Bloomberg Law). Disclosure schedules in Word with 12+ drafts. Only 5–10% of contracts examined.
→ Solution must transform unstructured content into queryable, cross-referenceable knowledge automatically.
P3 — Deal-Driven Timelines
DD timelines are set by deal dynamics, not by work volume.
30–90 day windows regardless of volume. Closure probability drops below 50% past 90 days (Business Sale Report). CEO/CFO at 30 hours/week (SilMinds). Deal duration up 48% despite technology improvements (Datasite).
→ Solution must compress analysis time to fit deal-driven windows. Speed is a hard constraint, not a nice-to-have.
P4 — Cross-Document Value
The analytical value in DD comes from connections between documents, not from any individual document in isolation — and no tool currently provides cross-document or cross-workstream synthesis.
84% of breach claims involve audited targets (Euclid). 80% of RWI losses from 3 cross-document categories (Aon). 8 case studies of missed cross-references. IC memo is the sole synthesis point (Stanford/Addepar).
→ Solution must enable cross-document analysis as a first-class capability. Information architecture must be relational, not per-document.
P5 — Structural Silo Failure
DD requires domain-specific expertise across multiple disciplines, structurally preventing synthesis — and organizational theory predicts this failure as inevitable.
8–10+ parallel workstreams. W&I claims reveal 5 most commonly missed cross-workstream interactions. 9/11 Commission: 23 missed opportunities from structural fragmentation (Zegart, 2007). I-PASS reduced handoff errors 30–47% (Starmer et al., NEJM).
→ Solution must provide a shared analytical layer that synthesizes across workstreams without requiring cross-domain expertise from any single user.
P6 — Confirmation Bias
Deal incentive structures create systematic bias toward confirming the investment thesis rather than detecting disconfirming evidence.
DD “seldom leads managers to kill deals, even deeply flawed ones” (HBR). Confirmation bias survives audit controls (Cassell et al., 2022). WMD Commission: hypothesis “no longer subject to scrutiny.” RWI claim rates spike after competitive markets (Fasken; SRS Acquiom).
→ System must surface disconfirming evidence prominently. Automated detection removes the incentive problem.

Jobs to Be Done

Functional

J1 — Document Prioritization
When reviewing a data room with thousands of documents under a tight deal deadline, identify which documents contain the key risks, terms, and obligations.
✓ Done when: most relevant documents surfaced and prioritized within hours
J2 — Cross-Document Verification
When finding a claim or figure in one document, verify it against every other relevant document in the data room to detect inconsistencies or anomalies.
✓ Done when: all cross-references identified; inconsistencies flagged with specific locations and page numbers
J3 — Cross-Workstream Synthesis
When multiple DD workstreams run in parallel, see what each team has found and whether findings connect or conflict.
✓ Done when: integrated view of all workstreams; no team’s blind spot becomes the deal’s blind spot
J4 — Portfolio Monitoring
When monitoring a portfolio company between formal reporting periods, know if something material has changed.
✓ Done when: real-time awareness of material changes as they happen, not months after
J5 — Sell-Side Readiness
When preparing for DD during a sale, have documents organized and information requests answered quickly.
✓ Done when: standard requests answered in minutes; management time measured in hours/week, not a second full-time job
J6 — Q&A Coordination
When answering buyer questions spanning multiple workstreams, route questions to the right people and track answers without Excel trackers and email chains.
✓ Done when: automated routing, duplicate detection, full audit trail; verbal findings integrated
