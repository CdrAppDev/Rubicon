# The crushing weight of sell-side due diligence in M&A

**Selling a company is, by every credible account, a second full-time job stacked on top of the first.** The sell-side due diligence burden in M&A transactions consumes hundreds of management hours across 6–12 months, exposes the target company to business performance decline, confidentiality breaches, and deal fatigue, and leaves the management team holding the bag for significant sunk costs if the deal fails. The evidence below — drawn from middle-market investment banks, law firms, data room providers, Big 4 accounting firms, and post-deal studies — paints a consistent picture of an extraordinarily demanding process that taxes every functional area of the target company.

---

## 1. The operational toll on target company management

The due diligence process demands **hundreds of hours** from the CEO, CFO, and their direct reports, effectively splitting senior leadership's attention between running the business and selling it. The CFO sits at the epicenter, quarterbackinng all financial information flows, while the CEO carries the narrative and strategic positioning burden.

**Finding 1.1**
- Claim: Due diligence during a business sale becomes "a second full-time job" for management. Diligence periods aim for 60 days but realistically take 90–120 days.

## 2. How sell-side advisors manage the diligence process

Middle-market investment banks serve as the **quarterback and gatekeeper** of the sell-side DD process. Their role involves building and populating data rooms from day one of engagement, managing "massive trackers" across diligence verticals, fielding buyer questions to shield management, and running a disciplined timeline — typically structured in four phases over roughly 16 weeks.

**Finding 2.1**
- Claim: Middle-market investment banks use "massive trackers" to coordinate the assembly and dissemination of information across due diligence verticals (legal, accounting, business, technology). The advisor anticipates buyer requests, identifies required documents, tracks what's gathered versus missing, and ensures timely delivery. They generally gather "a thousand or more documents" per process.
- Source: Jeff Guylay, Managing Director, Colonnade Advisors
- Date: Podcast Episode 004 (2020–2022 series)
- URL: https://coladv.com/podcasts/004/
- Quote: "It's our job to anticipate what buyers and their attorneys and counsel and consultants are going to look for in diligence... We manage this process and all these different verticals through these due diligence trackers. These are massive trackers where we're coordinating the assembly and ultimately dissemination of information."
- Relevance: Reveals the operational infrastructure required to manage sell-side DD — massive tracking systems with 1,000+ documents.

**Finding 2.2**
- Claim: Colonnade Advisors begins building the data room on the first day of engagement, well before going to market. They phase access strategically — a limited initial data room for ~20 buyers, then expanded access for ~6 serious bidders.
- Source: Jeff Guylay, Managing Director, Colonnade Advisors
- Date: Podcast Episode 025 (April 2022)
- URL: https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/middle-market-mergers-and-acquisitions-by-colonnade/id1521532548
- Quote: "The data room acts as a central repository of all the information that describes the company, its operations, history, and future. We start building this data room on day one of our engagement."
- Relevance: Data room construction is not a one-time event but an ongoing effort spanning the entire engagement.

**Finding 2.3**
- Claim: Providing substantial information in the data room before LOIs are due leads to more informed bids and reduces later price changes ("re-trades").
- Source: Gina Cocking, CEO, Colonnade Advisors

- Relevance: Strategic data room loading is a key advisor technique for reducing re-trade risk — shifting diligence burden earlier.

**Finding 2.4**
- Claim: The investment banker mediates all interaction between buyer and seller during DD. Founders should not engage directly with buyers. The banker sets the professional tone, acts as "bad cop," and manages information flow to preserve negotiation leverage.

**Finding 2.5**
- Claim: The middle-market sell-side M&A process is organized into four phases over approximately 16 weeks: Pre-Marketing, Go to Market, Management Presentations/Buyer DD, and Exclusivity/Documentation. "Time is the enemy."
- Source: Colonnade Advisors

- Relevance: Establishes the tight timeline that advisors manage — and the inverse relationship between process duration and deal success.

## 3. Building the data room is a months-long ordeal

Data room preparation begins **2–3 months before going to market** (longer for complex businesses), involves an internal team of roughly **6 people**, and results in a repository of **1,000+ documents** spanning 14+ categories. Common failures include outdated documents, missing records, over-disclosure, and inconsistent access permissions.

**Finding 3.1**
- Claim: The minimum preparation period for an M&A data room is 2–3 months if the business has no substantial changes; if changes exist, preparation may require 6+ months. The average overall M&A process timeline is 9+ months.
- Source: Ansarada
- Date: Current
- URL: https://www.ansarada.com/mergers-acquisitions/process
- Quote: "The minimum preparation period for an M&A transaction is 2 to 3 months if there are no substantial changes to the sell-side business or its reporting structure. If there are changes, both internal and external advisors may need 6 months or longer for deal preparation."
- Relevance: Data room construction alone can consume a quarter or more of total deal time.

**Finding 3.2**
- Claim: Middle-market sell-side data rooms typically contain **over 1,000 documents**. Each sub-segment of DD can have 100+ categories with dozens of documents within each.
- Source: Jeff Guylay, Managing Director, Colonnade Advisors

- URL: https://coladv.com/podcasts/004/
- Quote: "Each sub-segment can have a hundred plus categories, and there can be dozens of documents within each. We generally gather a thousand or more documents in each due diligence process for a company."
- Relevance: Quantifies the sheer volume of documentation that must be gathered, organized, reviewed, and uploaded.

**Finding 3.3**
- Claim: A comprehensive M&A legal due diligence request list encompasses at least **174 distinct types of documents**, spanning corporate governance, financial, legal, tax, IP, environmental, and operational categories.
- Source: Bloomberg Law
- Date: Current
- URL: https://pro.bloomberglaw.com/insights/contracts/ma-due-diligence-checklist/

**Finding 3.4**

- Source: V7 Labs

- Quote: "A multi-jurisdictional, multi-billion dollar merger will involve tens of thousands of documents and rigorous compliance checks."

**Finding 3.5**

## 4. Vendor due diligence is reshaping the sell-side playbook

Vendor due diligence (VDD) — where the seller commissions its own pre-emptive diligence reports before going to market — is **standard practice in European M&A** and gaining traction in the US. VDD costs **£100,000–£200,000** but can reduce buyer DD scope, accelerate deal timelines, and eliminate late-stage surprises. All Big 4 firms actively market VDD services across financial, tax, commercial, legal, IT, HR, operational, and ESG disciplines.

**Finding 4.1**
- Claim: VDD emerged in the early 2000s and became standard for mid-market deals by the 2010s. It is now increasingly prevalent further down the deal size spectrum. "Should now be considered by any serious seller."
- Source: Jim Shaw, Shaw & Co (UK M&A advisory firm)
- Date: January 21, 2026
- URL: https://www.shawcorporatefinance.com/connected/vendor-due-diligence-what-you-need-to-know
- Quote: "VDD emerged in the early 2000s as private equity and corporate acquisitions grew more complex. Buyers demanded deeper insights, slowing deals and increasing costs. Sellers began using VDD to take control, providing transparent data upfront to accelerate negotiations and, importantly, eliminating late surprises."
- Relevance: VDD is the sell-side's primary tool for shifting the DD burden earlier in the process and controlling the narrative.

**Finding 4.2**
- Claim: VDD costs typically range from **£100,000 to £200,000** depending on business size, report scope, and complexity. Market practice has traditionally been that VDD costs are paid by the winning buyer, though recent push-back has some buyers insisting it is a seller cost.
- Source: Shaw & Co
- Date: January 21, 2026
- URL: https://www.shawcorporatefinance.com/connected/vendor-due-diligence-what-you-need-to-know
- Quote: "The upfront cost, typically ranging from £100,000 to £200,000 depending on business size, report scope and complexity, is a significant investment. Worst-case scenario? You complete VDD, pay the fees, and don't receive an acceptable offer."
- Relevance: VDD represents a significant upfront investment that is at risk if the deal doesn't close.

**Finding 4.3**
- Claim: VDD reports have been "widespread and routine" in European jurisdictions for several years. They are "now becoming less of an anomaly in the United States." In Europe, it's called "vendor due diligence"; in the US, "sell-side due diligence."
- Source: Blake, Cassels & Graydon LLP (Blakes)
- Date: Recent
- URL: https://www.blakes.com/getmedia/C750783C-62D0-43DC-A3B5-656F51DDDA49/Legal_Vendor_Due_Diligence.aspx
- Quote: "In the context of private equity and auction sales, VDD reports have been widespread and routine in European jurisdictions for several years. They are now becoming less of an anomaly in the United States."
- Relevance: Establishes the geographic adoption pattern — Europe leads, US follows.

**Finding 4.4**
- Claim: All Big 4 firms actively market VDD services spanning financial, tax, operational, commercial, IT, HR/pensions, ESG, and cyber due diligence. PwC states VDD provides "greater control over the sale process" and can "help secure a higher price." KPMG notes that once the VDD report is presented, "the buyer is required to carry out only limited additional due diligence."
- Source: Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, EY (service pages)
- Date: Current
- URL: Various (Deloitte CH, PwC UK, KPMG PL/DK, EY Global)
- Quote (PwC): "Provide vendors with greater control over the sale process and the timing of sale, which can help secure a higher price." / Quote (KPMG): "Once the vendor presents the VDD report to the buyer, the buyer is required to carry out only limited additional due diligence."
- Relevance: The Big 4's endorsement of VDD signals institutional acceptance that seller-initiated diligence reduces the overall burden.

**Finding 4.5**
- Claim: VDD reports serve as a foundation for buyer DD, reducing its scope and cost. Buyers may conduct only limited "confirmatory" or "top-up" due diligence after reviewing VDD. This lowers cost of participation for bidders, enables more bidders to participate, and reduces management time burden.
- Source: Ansarada / S&W Group / KPMG Denmark
- Date: Various
- URL: https://www.ansarada.com/article/business-readiness-transactional-vendor-due-diligence-reports / https://www.swgroup.com/services/corporate-finance-and-transactions/transaction-services/vendor-due-diligence-and-vendor-assistance-services/
- Quote (KPMG Denmark): "Once the vendor presents the VDD report to the buyer, the buyer is required to carry out only limited additional due diligence."
- Relevance: VDD's core value proposition is converting full buyer DD into limited confirmatory DD — dramatically reducing management involvement.

## 5. Confidentiality is under siege throughout the process

Maintaining confidentiality during a sale process is a core challenge: **9.5% of global M&A deals leaked in 2024** — a 16-year high. Target companies must balance buyer demands for detailed information against the risk of empowering a competitor, triggering employee unrest, or alerting customers and suppliers. The defenses include NDAs, tiered data rooms, staged disclosure, clean teams, document watermarking, and code names.

**Finding 5.1**
- Claim: **9.5% of global M&A deals leaked in 2024**, the highest figure in the 16-year history of the SS&C Intralinks/Bayes Business School study. Leaked deals took roughly two additional weeks to complete on average.
- Source: SS&C Intralinks and Bayes Business School, City, University of London
- Date: 2025 (covering 2024 data)
- URL: https://www.intralinks.com/resources/blog/why-ma-leaks-hit-16-year-high
- Quote: "The study shows that 9.5 percent of global M&A deals leaked in 2024, a notable rise from the previous year and the highest figure recorded in the study's 16-year history."
- Relevance: Nearly 1 in 10 deals leaks — a staggering confidentiality failure rate for a process built on secrecy.

**Finding 5.2**
- Claim: Clean teams — neutral bodies operating under strict confidentiality protocols — have shifted from optional to essential in M&A. They filter competitively sensitive information (CSI) including customer information, pricing/profitability data, production costs, and utilization data.
- Source: McKinsey & Company
- Date: Recent
- URL: https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/deal-delays-are-the-new-normal-clean-teams-are-the-fix
- Quote: "Today, amid extended deal timelines, clean teams have shifted from being optional—and often diligence focused—to being essential."
- Relevance: Extended DD timelines increase the window for leaks, making clean teams more critical and more expensive to maintain.

**Finding 5.3**
- Claim: Best practice is a phased approach to disclosing personal data (employee, customer) during M&A. Early stage: no names or contact details — anonymized/pseudonymized. Due diligence stage: gradually disclose management names, key employee details. Final bid stage: disclose data on all key employees and unmasked customer data.
- Source: Wilson Sonsini Goodrich & Rosati (Lore Leitner and Elli Laine)
- Date: March 2019
- URL: https://www.wsgr.com/PDFSearch/personal-data-disclosure-0319.pdf
- Quote: "At an early stage the target should not provide the names or contact details of its employees or customers. Personal data should be removed or masked using anonymization or pseudonymization techniques."
- Relevance: Staged disclosure adds work — each round of buyer access requires re-redaction and re-permissioning of the data room.

**Finding 5.4**
- Claim: If a deal falls apart after DD, the target may have created a better-informed competitor with "a unique view of the target's strengths and weaknesses, strategies and assets."
- Source: Procopio (law firm)
- Date: Current
- URL: https://www.procopio.com/resource/3-steps-protect-assets-in-deal
- Quote: "If the deal falls apart, the target could have enabled or even created a formidable competitor with a unique view of the target's strengths and weaknesses, strategies and assets."
- Relevance: The confidentiality risk is existential — a failed deal with a competitor-buyer may permanently damage competitive position.

**Finding 5.5**
- Claim: A comprehensive confidentiality management framework includes: tiered data rooms with staged disclosure, customer name redaction until later rounds, watermarked documents with user-specific identifiers, view-only/time-limited permissions, login activity monitoring, aggressive NDA enforcement, internal/external code names, tight "circle of trust," and pre-drafted crisis communication plans.
- Source: PCE Companies
- Date: Recent (references 2024 data)
- URL: https://www.pcecompanies.com/resources/managing-confidentiality-ma-sale
- Quote: "Confidentiality isn't paperwork — it's a shield that protects your valuation, your people, and your future options."
- Relevance: The confidentiality burden is itself an operational workstream requiring active management throughout the deal.

## 6. Management presentations consume weeks of executive calendar

Target company executives typically deliver **management presentations to 4–6 shortlisted buyers**, one per day, each lasting several hours to a full day. The presentation phase spans **4–6 weeks** and requires the CEO, CFO, COO, and functional leaders to present a 50+ slide deck, answer intense questioning, and conduct facility tours. Preparation and delivery together block approximately two weeks of solid calendar time.

**Finding 6.1**
- Claim: Management teams should expect to "block calendars for management presentations" for approximately two weeks. Format is one bidder per day, presenting a 50+ slide deck. Management presentations represent "only the halfway point" of the process — the intensive DD period follows.
- Source: Ben Murray, The SaaS CFO
- Date: Ongoing
- URL: https://www.thesaascfo.com/ma-management-presentations/
- Quote: "The banker said to block my calendar for management presentations. Excuse me, what are management presentations? And for two weeks? Yes, and this is only the halfway point. The real 'fun' in due diligence comes after the management presentations."
- Relevance: Two weeks of blocked executive calendars is just the midpoint — confirmatory DD follows for 8–12 more weeks.

**Finding 6.2**
- Claim: Management presentations typically occur with a curated group of **4–6 serious buyers**. Each presentation lasts from "a few hours to a full day." Includes introductions of senior team, presentations by each executive on their area (Operations, Finance, Quality, HR), Q&A, and facility tour.
- Source: Divestopedia / Aldermanco
- Date: Various
- URL: https://www.divestopedia.com/definition/933/management-presentation/ / https://www.aldermanco.com/deal-notes/management-presentations/
- Quote: "Management presentations usually occur after the expression of interest (EOI) is received and can be conducted with numerous buyers. The presentation can last from a few hours to a full day."
- Relevance: Each buyer gets a full-day session — with 4–6 buyers, that's nearly a full week of management time just for delivery.

**Finding 6.3**
- Claim: The management presentation phase takes 4–6 weeks with "high" management involvement. The finance section — led by the CFO — is "subject to the most intense scrutiny."
- Source: Sampford Advisors / 8020 Consulting
- Date: April 2025 / March 2020
- URL: https://www.sampfordadvisors.com/blog/2025/2/28/sell-side-process-timeline / https://8020consulting.com/blog/sell-side-finance-ma-management

- Relevance: The CFO bears disproportionate presentation burden because buyers scrutinize financials most intensely.

## 7. Information request fatigue compounds with each additional bidder

A comprehensive M&A process involves **7–12 parallel due diligence workstreams**, each run by different specialist teams generating separate request lists. A single buyer's DD can produce **hundreds of individual line items**; with 10–20 active buyers simultaneously asking questions, the target faces an avalanche of overlapping, duplicative, and escalating requests. Different workstreams frequently request the same information from the target, compounding the burden.

**Finding 7.1**
- Claim: A comprehensive M&A DD process involves multiple parallel workstreams including: Financial/Accounting, Legal, Tax, Commercial, HR/People, IT/Technology, Cybersecurity/Data Privacy, Operations/Synergies, Environmental, Insurance, Regulatory, and sometimes ESG. Each workstream generates its own request list.
- Source: EY
- Date: Current

**Finding 7.2**

## 8. Business performance visibly suffers during the sale process

The evidence is unambiguous: target companies experience **measurable operational degradation** during DD periods. Management attention drifts, customers erode, staff turn over, and financial projections get missed. A performance dip during DD directly threatens valuation and can kill the deal — creating a vicious feedback loop where the process designed to close the deal undermines the business that makes the deal worth closing.

**Finding 8.1**
- Claim: "Selling a business can be a full-time job" that competes with running the business. "Selling your business and running your business are two full-time jobs. Something is bound to slip at the wrong time."

## Conclusion: A system that punishes the sell-side at every turn

The sell-side due diligence burden in M&A is a structural problem, not an execution problem. **Even well-run processes** consume hundreds of management hours, span 6–12 months, generate 1,000+ documents across 7–12 parallel workstreams, and put CEO and CFO time at **30 hours per week** during peak periods. The system's design creates cascading pain points: multiple bidders multiply information requests; DD timelines are lengthening (45 days → 60–90 days post-LOI); confidentiality breaches hit a 16-year high at 9.5% of deals; and roughly **10% of large deals die** after the target has invested $500K+ and thousands of management hours.

Three dynamics stand out as particularly punishing. First, the **deal distraction death spiral** — management attention diverted to DD causes business performance to decline, which buyers then use to re-trade price, which extends the process further. Second, the **information asymmetry in experience** — buyers do dozens of deals while sellers typically do one, giving buyers a structural advantage in using DD as a negotiation lever. Third, the **cost asymmetry** — legal and accounting fees are incurred regardless of outcome while investment banking success fees are contingent, meaning the seller absorbs most of the sunk cost risk.

Vendor due diligence represents the most significant countermeasure, converting extensive buyer DD into limited confirmatory reviews and reducing management exposure. VDD is already standard in European PE auctions and growing in the US. But at £100,000–£200,000 per engagement, it merely shifts the timing and nature of the burden rather than eliminating it. The fundamental challenge remains: **selling a company requires operating two full-time jobs simultaneously**, and the evidence shows that something always gives.
