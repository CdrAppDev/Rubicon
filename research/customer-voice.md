# Customer voice evidence validates every due diligence pain hypothesis

**All six hypotheses find support from practitioner voices, though evidence strength varies significantly.** H1 (manual review burden), H3 (information search friction), H4 (team fragmentation), and H6 (founder time burden) are overwhelmingly validated by independent, emotionally charged practitioner accounts across Wall Street Oasis, Glassdoor, G2/Capterra reviews, and practitioner blogs. H2 (missed risk signals) and H5 (portfolio monitoring gaps) have moderate support but rely more on industry-framed sources than raw first-person accounts. The strongest recurring pattern across all sources is **resigned exhaustion** — practitioners describe due diligence not as occasionally painful but as *structurally* broken, with workarounds (spreadsheets, late nights, dedicated hires for grunt work) treated as permanent fixtures rather than temporary measures.

## Unanticipated findings that extend beyond the six hypotheses

Several patterns emerged from the research that **no hypothesis directly anticipated:**

**The "performative diligence" problem.** Multiple WSO quotes describe DD activity that exists to *demonstrate* thoroughness rather than to *achieve* it — "100+ page info packs" for investment committees, rework invented by competitive VPs, and DD processes that continue even when findings don't support proceeding. This suggests a significant portion of DD effort is **theater**, not analysis, which inflates the burden without improving outcomes.

**VDR tools solve security but create new problems.** The Capterra/G2 evidence consistently shows that existing data room platforms solved the physical access and security problem but **inadvertently created information overload**. As V7 Labs described it: "A VDR often becomes a passive digital filing cabinet." The tools don't help users *understand* documents — they merely *store* them securely. This is a platform-level gap, not a feature request.

**The "two-sided pain" dynamic.** H6 focuses on the founder's burden, but WSO quotes from the banker/advisor side reveal that **the pain is mirrored** — advisors find working with founders on DD equally frustrating ("They are never happy...3 weeks easily turns into 6 weeks"). This suggests an AI solution would need to serve both sides of the table to capture full value.

**Cultural normalization of suffering.** Across all sources, the most striking meta-pattern is **resigned acceptance**. Practitioners describe DD pain not as an anomaly to be solved but as an inherent feature of the profession. "It's still going to suck" is treated as wisdom rather than a call to action. This normalization creates a marketing challenge — potential users may not actively seek solutions for pain they've been taught to simply endure.

## Conclusion: What the evidence reveals about building an AI DD platform

The strongest signal in this research isn't any single quote — it's the **convergence of independent voices across platforms, roles, and years all describing the same structural problems**. Investment banking analysts on WSO, Big Law attorneys on Glassdoor, data room users on Capterra, PE consultants on forums, and founders on blogs all independently describe overlapping frustrations without any awareness of each other's complaints. This convergence is the highest-confidence signal that these problems are real, widespread, and persistent.

**H1 and H3 represent the most acute, daily pain** — they are experienced as constant friction by every person who touches DD documents. **H4 is the most structurally complex problem** — it involves organizational design, not just tooling. **H6 is the most emotionally resonant problem** — founders describe DD in survival language. **H2 is the highest-stakes problem** — missed risks cause the most expensive failures — but it is the least self-reported because professionals don't publicize their misses. **H5 is the most chronic but least acute problem** — it matters enormously but generates less emotional urgency.

The evidence suggests the root cause underlying all six hypotheses is a single structural failure: **due diligence remains fundamentally a manual, document-by-document, human-reading process despite operating at a scale that exceeds human capacity within deal timelines.** Every hypothesis describes a downstream consequence of this core mismatch between volume and capacity. An AI platform that addresses the root cause — making large document sets queryable, cross-referenceable, and intelligently summarized — would simultaneously address all six pain points rather than treating them as separate problems.
